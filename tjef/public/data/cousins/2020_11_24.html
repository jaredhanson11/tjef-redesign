Jared Hanson
Member of our Board of Governors
 
The John Ernest Foundation is quite proud of its Board of Governors, and this week we are featuring Board member Jared Hanson.
 
Beginning his stint with TJEF as a high school sophomore spending a summer in Lima (mentored by Ryan Esposto), until his present, significant work as a member of TJEF’s Board of Governors, Jared is the perfect TJEF story. After Peru, it was the International Summer Science School in Heidelberg. Then an MIT quarterback (where just before graduating, he accompanied his professor to read a paper at the Technical University of Delft, in The Netherlands). Apple first, now a startup.
 
He says of TJEF, "I was introduced as a TJEF fellow when I was a freshman in high school and experienced first hand the organization’s impact on my life. When I was asked to join the board after graduating from MIT, it was a no brainer. This organization, comprised of primarily volunteers, has such a large impact on hundreds of individuals who come from from all walks of life. It’s special to see the ways TJEF organically develops its fellows by connecting them with mentors within TJEF’s network or providing funds to help the fellow pursue a dream, all the while continuing to push the fellow outside of his or her comfort zone to enable personal growth."

Positive Grandparenting
TJEF History
 
TJEF Yesteryear: this became a darling project for many members of the Board of Governors at the time.  From their June 1996 sendout:
 
Positive Grandparenting
San Jose, California
 
A greater percentage of children are living with grandparents or other relatives than at any time since the Great Depression, Grandparent caregiver-headed households are generally the poorest financially of all nontraditional family types. These grandparents feel the effects of stress due to frustration, worry over legal issues, problems with housing, and emotional and health concerns. Many are single grandmothers who have to work to support themselves and their grandchildren. Recent statistics indicate that the numbers are continuing to rise. The largest cause for this rise is the drug epidemic. Innocent children are often the victims of their parents’ substance abuse and the extended family is stressed to deal with the situation.
 
Positive Grandparenting is a volunteer support group of grandparents helping other grandparents to cope with the complex issues regarding their grandchildren. It also works toward positive solutions to overcome some of the obstacles and to put more emphasis on the need to protect children,
 
A June 5, 1996 article in the San Jose Mercury News states that it is difficult to get foster parents because “the children coming into the system are much more needy than in past years.” The grand jury made 10) recommendations to reduce foster parent dropout, “including initiating a respite program for foster parents, subsidizing a foster parent association, finding free legal services for foster parents, and establishing two part-time foster parent advocates.” The foster parents only have to deal with the children’s issues while the grandparents also have to contend with the legal system, their relationship with the parents, and their own financial and health needs.
 
Public services need to be extended to address the needs of grandparent caregiver families and to recognize the important role that they play. Some of the areas of need are children and adult counseling, respite care, financial assistance, housing, education, subsidized day care, and legal assistance.
 
We also have social functions so the grandparents and grandchildren can have fun together and we publish a newsletter to keep our members informed about activities and topics of interest.
 
The support group provides a safe place where grandparents can unburden themselves and receive support from other grandparents who have been there. The emphasis is on how best to protect and care for our grandchildren. The group is diverse with a wide spectrum of complex situations. The following are a few examples:
 
 - They may be caring for their grandchildren through an informal agreement with         the parents, in which case they have no legal rights or financial assistance. The           parents can take the children any time and frequently hold this threat over the           grandparents. The legal expense and fear of having the children returned to               unfit parents often keeps these grandparents from taking legal action.
 
 - Many grandparents are the safety net for children who have been abused and             neglected by their parents.
 
 - Teenage pregnancy and divorce puts many grandparents into the situation of               having the responsibility of caring for their grandchildren. One of the parents             may live with them because they lack the maturity and stability to care for their         children. 

 - Their grandchildren may be suffering emotional and physical abuse with their             parents and the grandparents are limited in what they can do to help them.
 
 - Then there are the day-to-day issues of raising children, many of whom are                 physically and emotionally damaged, and all of the changes and adjustments in           the grandparents lives.
 
One of the most frustrating things for grandparents is that they can’t exercise common sense and intervene when they find their grandchildren in a detrimental situation. Almost without exception, the grandparents get caught in a sea of legal issues that are financially and emotionally draining.
 
The John Ernest Foundation, a mentoring foundation, offered their assistance in carly 1996 and are helping us to realize our dream to better serve the needs of the grandchildren and grandparents in our area. Through their assistance, we have applied to become a nonprofit public benefit corporation.
